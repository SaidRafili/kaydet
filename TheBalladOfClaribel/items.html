Why Was I Looking Out?

The cover of the sheet music for "Why Was I Looking Out?", an 1866 song. Lyrics are by Claribel, and they're essentially the same as those for "Through the Jessamine," but from the perspective of the other character in the story. The music for this one is by Jacques Blumenthal, and the cover bears his royalty signature of JB.

Claribel

Boosey and Co., London

1866

Through the Jessamine

The cover of the sheet music for Claribel's song "Through the Jessamine," as well as the lyrics, which were published separately in her posthumous volume Thoughts, Verses, and Songs (1877). "Through the Jessamine" was first published in Boosey and Sons' St. James's Album for 1863, which hit stands at the end of 1862 judging by Illustrated London News advertisements, but it was also released as a separate piece of sheet music sometime later. Another item herein, "Why Was I Looking Out?", is not so much a sequel to this song as a retelling of the same story from the other party.

Claribel

Boosey and Sons, London (music); James Nisbet and Co., London (poetry book)

1862 (music), 1877 (poetry book)

The Broken Sixpence

The cover of the sheet music for Claribel's 1862 song "The Broken Sixpence." This song is an answer to "The Blue Ribbon," and the two songs were released at the same time.

Claribel

Boosey and Sons, London

1862

The Blue Ribbon

The illustrated cover of the sheet music for Claribel's 1862 song The Blue Ribbon. The original art likely featured a blue ribbon with a gold shadow, though the blue ink has seemingly since degraded to a pale dusty bluish color.

Claribel

Boosey and Sons, London

1862

I Love [Like] to Sing the Old Songs

Screenshots of the cover and an excerpt from a song variously titled "I Love to Sing the Old Songs" and "I Like to Sing the Old Songs" - and by "variously titled" I do mean both of those titles are used on the same piece of sheet music. The words and music are by Charles Hodgson. Though the poetic style of the lyrics makes it obvious that this song is an answer to/riff off Claribel's "I Cannot Sing the Old Songs," the melody actually bears no resemblance to the original, unlike a fair few sequels or riffs of this era.

Charles Hodgson

Charles W. Jordan, Baltimore, MD

between 1867 and 1873, per Hathi Trust

What Need Have I the Truth to Tell?

The cover of the sheet music for "What Need Have I the Truth to Tell?", Claribel's official reply to her 1861 song "Won't You Tell Me Why, Robin?" Additionally, two versions of the same lyric are included here: the first from the official sheet music, and the second from an American reprint published by Wm. A. Pond.

Claribel

Boosey & Co., London

That's the Reason Why, Darling

The cover of the sheet music for "That's the Reason Why, Darling," an unofficial sequel to Claribel's "Won't You Tell Me Why, Robin?" One of only a few I've found so far to come from England rather than the United States. The lyricist for this song is Blanche Barnard, who I don't believe is any relation to Claribel herself (Charlotte Alington Barnard), or more properly to Claribel's husband Charles Cary Barnard

Blanche Barnard (lyricist); Ernest Donajowski (composer)

H. D'Alcorn & Co., London

1868

The Folly of Wooing (cover and excerpt)

The sheet music for "The Folly of Wooing," an unofficial reply to Claribel's "Five O'Clock in the Morning." The text is by C.H. Webb, and it was also used for "It's All Very Well." The music here is by W.B.H.

Included are the front cover and an excerpt from the first page. The text reads as follows: "It is all very well for the poets to tell / By way of their songs adorning, / Of milkmaids who rouse to manipulate cows, At five o'clock in the morning."

C.H. Webb (lyricist); W.B.H. (composer)

Fischer & Bro, Dayton

1869
